Grammar — Complete Rules

All error-spotting and sentence correction patterns tested in TCS TQT

TCS TQT tests 8–10 grammar questions per exam. SVA, tenses, modifiers, and parallelism are the highest-frequency topics.
⚡ Subject-Verb Agreement
  • Collective nouns (team, committee, jury, staff, government, army) → singular verb.
    "The team is performing well."
  • Indefinite pronouns (everyone, someone, nobody, each, either, neither) → always singular.
    "Neither of the answers is correct."
  • Or / Nor rule: verb agrees with the nearest subject.
    "Neither the students nor the teacher was present."
  • "And" rule: usually plural, except when the two subjects refer to one concept.
    "Bread and butter is my breakfast." (one item)
  • Intervening phrases (along with, as well as, together with, besides) do NOT change subject.
    "The manager, along with his team, has arrived."
  • Relative pronoun verb agrees with the antecedent.
    "She is one of those students who work hard." (antecedent = students)
TCS exam pattern
✗ Each of the boys have completed the task.
✓ Each of the boys has completed the task.
"Each" is always singular → use "has"
🕐 Tenses — Key Rules
  • Simple past vs. present perfect: Use simple past with specific time (yesterday, last year, in 2020). Use present perfect with unspecified time (already, just, ever, never, since, for).
    "I have lived here for 5 years." / "I moved here in 2019."
  • Past perfect: For the earlier of two past actions → had + V3.
    "She had left before I arrived."
  • Conditionals:
    Type 1 (real): If + simple present → will + base
    Type 2 (hypothetical): If + simple past → would + base
    Type 3 (impossible): If + past perfect → would have + V3
  • Reported speech: Tenses shift back one step.
    "She said she was tired." (not "is")
  • Stative verbs (know, believe, understand, love, hate, seem, appear, own, contain) → never use continuous form.
    "I know the answer." (not "am knowing")
✗ I have met him yesterday at the station.
✓ I met him yesterday at the station.
"Yesterday" is a specific time → use simple past
📖 Articles — A / An / The
  • Use "a" before consonant sounds: a university (y-sound), a European, a one-time offer
  • Use "an" before vowel sounds: an hour (silent h), an MBA, an honest person
  • Use "the" with: superlatives, unique things (the sun), specific known items, rivers/oceans/ranges, newspapers
  • No article with: languages, meals, diseases in general (has cancer — but: the flu, a cold), plural general nouns (Dogs are loyal)
  • The + adjective = a class of people: the poor, the rich, the elderly, the blind
✗ She is best student in the class.
✓ She is the best student in the class.
Superlatives always take "the"
🔗 Modifiers — Dangling & Misplaced
  • Dangling modifier: The modifier's subject is absent from the sentence. Fix by adding the correct subject.
    Wrong: "Walking down the street, the rain began to fall." (Rain can't walk!)
    Fix: "Walking down the street, I felt the rain begin to fall."
  • Misplaced modifier: Too far from the word it modifies.
    Wrong: "She almost drove her children to school every day." (She never did?)
    Fix: "She drove her children to school almost every day."
  • "Only" must sit immediately before the word it modifies. Every position changes meaning:
    "Only I told him." / "I only told him." / "I told only him."
✗ Exhausted from the long journey, the bed seemed very inviting.
✓ Exhausted from the long journey, she found the bed very inviting.
The bed cannot be exhausted — the subject of the modifier must match the main clause subject
⚖️ Parallelism
  • In lists: All elements must share the same grammatical form.
    Wrong: "She likes reading, writing, and to sing."
    Correct: "She likes reading, writing, and singing."
  • Correlative conjunctions (both…and, either…or, neither…nor, not only…but also): same structure must follow both conjunctions.
    Wrong: "Not only did he win the race but also he broke the record."
    Correct: "Not only did he win the race but also break the record."
  • Comparisons: Compare like with like.
    Wrong: "Her salary is higher than her colleague." (salary ≠ person)
    Correct: "Her salary is higher than her colleague's."

👤 Pronoun Rules
  • Pronoun must agree with its antecedent in number and gender. "Each student must bring his or her own book."
  • Use who for people, which for things, that for both in defining clauses.
  • Use whom (object form): "To whom should I address this letter?"
  • Reflexive pronouns only when subject = object: "I hurt myself." — NOT "Please contact myself."
  • "Between you and me" (not "I") — after prepositions, always use object pronouns.
🔄 Voice — Active vs. Passive (Sentence Correction)
  • Passive: Be + V3 (past participle). "The letter was written by him."
  • In passive, the object of the active sentence becomes the subject.
  • Agent (doer) is introduced by by. If unimportant, the agent is often dropped.
  • Modals in passive: modal + be + V3. "The work must be completed today."
✗ The cake was ate by the children.
✓ The cake was eaten by the children.
"Ate" is simple past; passive requires past participle "eaten"

⚠️ Most Confused Word Pairs
  • Affect vs Effect: Affect = verb (to influence). Effect = noun (result). "The noise affected my concentration. The effect was severe."
  • Complement vs Compliment: Complement = completes/matches. Compliment = praise. "The sauce complements the dish."
  • Principal vs Principle: Principal = head/main. Principle = a rule or belief.
  • Elicit vs Illicit: Elicit = draw out. Illicit = illegal.
  • Eminent vs Imminent: Eminent = famous. Imminent = about to happen.
  • Flaunt vs Flout: Flaunt = show off. Flout = openly disregard rules.
  • Lay vs Lie: Lay = to put (needs object). Lie = to recline (no object).
  • Fewer vs Less: Fewer = countable (fewer students). Less = uncountable (less water).
  • Infer vs Imply: Imply = suggest (speaker does it). Infer = conclude (listener does it).

Para Jumbles — Strategy & Practice

A 5-step system to crack any para jumble in under 90 seconds

Para jumbles test logical thinking, not English grammar. Learn the signals — they appear in every question without exception.
🗺️ The 5-Step Solving Method
1
Find the mandatory FIRST sentence. It introduces the topic with no back-reference. It does NOT start with: pronouns (he/she/they/it/this/these/such), contrast words (however/but/yet), result words (therefore/hence/thus), or addition words (also/moreover).
2
Find the mandatory LAST sentence. It concludes the argument. Often starts with: "thus", "therefore", "in conclusion", "finally", "hence", "as a result", "ultimately".
3
Find pronoun-antecedent pairs. A sentence starting with He/She/They/It/This/These/Such must come AFTER the sentence that introduces the noun it refers to. These are forced-pair links.
4
Use connectors as bridges. Match cause → effect (because/so/thus), contrast (however/but/yet/nevertheless), addition (moreover/furthermore/also), sequence (then/next/subsequently), illustration (for example/such as).
5
Verify end-to-end. Read your complete sequence. Does it flow naturally? Any abrupt jump? If yes, re-examine your pair links at that break point.
🚫 A Sentence is NEVER the First if it starts with…
  • "This / These / Such / That / Those" — always refers to something previously mentioned
  • "He / She / They / It" — the noun hasn't been introduced yet
  • "However / But / Yet / Nevertheless / Although" — contrast needs something to contrast with
  • "Therefore / Hence / Thus / Consequently / As a result" — a result needs a cause first
  • "Also / Moreover / Furthermore / Besides / In addition" — addition needs something to add to
  • "For example / For instance / Such as / To illustrate" — an example needs a claim to illustrate

✅ Solved Example — Step by Step
Arrange sentences A–E in the correct order:
A. This led to widespread deforestation and soil erosion in many regions.
B. Environmental scientists have warned that the situation is now irreversible in several areas.
C. For centuries, humans have relied on forests for timber, fuel, and agricultural land.
D. As the global population grew, the demand for these resources increased exponentially.
E. Governments must therefore act immediately to restore protected forest cover.
Step 1 — C has no back-reference, introduces the topic → C is first.
Step 2 — D says "demand increased" — consequence of reliance in C → D follows C.
Step 3 — A starts with "This" referring to increased demand (D) → A follows D.
Step 4 — B says "scientists warned situation irreversible" — result of deforestation (A) → B follows A.
Step 5 — E starts with "therefore" and calls for action — conclusion → E is last.
Answer: C → D → A → B → E
💡 Odd Sentence Out — Strategy
  • Find the central theme of the paragraph from 4 of the 5 sentences. The odd sentence breaks the theme.
  • A sentence introducing a new, unrelated person, concept, or example is almost always the odd one out.
  • Tonal shift: If 4 sentences are formal and 1 is colloquial (or vice versa), the tonal outlier is odd.
  • Time shift: If 4 sentences discuss the present and 1 switches to a historical event unrelated to the argument, that's the odd one.

Reading Comprehension + Cloze Test

Strategy, question types, two full passages, and cloze test practice

The answer is ALWAYS inside the passage. Never use outside knowledge. Every inference must be traceable to specific words in the text.
🧭 Active Reading Strategy — 5 Steps
1
Skim questions first (20 sec). Know what you're looking for before reading. Underline keywords in each question.
2
Read actively. For each paragraph, underline the topic sentence and note the main idea in 3–4 words in the margin.
3
Build a passage map. Para 1 = intro/context. Middle = evidence/arguments. Last = conclusion/author's stance.
4
Eliminate wrong options. Cross out: too extreme (always/never), outside scope, opposite of what's said, partially correct.
5
Verify your answer by locating the exact line in the passage that supports it. If you can't find it, it's wrong.
❓ All Question Types + How to Answer Each
Main Idea / Central Theme▶

"What is the passage mainly about?" / "The primary purpose of the passage is…"

Strategy: The correct answer covers the WHOLE passage. Eliminate options that are too narrow (one paragraph's topic) or too broad (barely mentioned).

✗ Too specific: "The passage is about AI bias in hiring"
✗ Too broad: "The passage is about technology"
✓ Correct: "The passage discusses AI's ethical challenges and the need for governance"
Inference questions▶

"It can be inferred that…" / "The author implies…" / "Which can be concluded?"

Strategy: Answer goes one logical step beyond what's stated but must be supported by the passage. If an option is directly stated, it's a FACT, not an inference. Do not pick options needing assumptions the passage doesn't support.

Tone / Attitude questions▶

"The author's tone is…" / "The attitude of the author towards X is…"

Key tone words:
Positive: optimistic, laudatory, admiring, appreciative, enthusiastic, sanguine
Negative: critical, sceptical, pessimistic, condemning, censorious, sardonic
Neutral: objective, analytical, detached, impartial, informative, dispassionate
Mixed: ambivalent, cautious, cautiously optimistic, wistful

Vocabulary-in-context▶

"As used in paragraph 2, the word 'X' most nearly means…"

Strategy: Substitute each option into the original sentence. The correct one fits the tone and meaning of the sentence — not just the dictionary definition of the word. A word like "grave" means serious, not a burial place, in most contexts.

Specific detail / NOT questions▶

"According to the passage, which of the following is true?" / "Which is NOT mentioned?"

Strategy: Locate the relevant paragraph from your passage map. For NOT questions — many students miss the word "not". Always re-read the question carefully before eliminating options.

📄 Passage 1 — Artificial Intelligence & Ethics
Artificial intelligence is no longer a concept confined to science fiction; it has become an integral part of modern industry and daily life. From personalised content recommendations to medical diagnosis assistance, AI systems are making decisions that were once exclusively human. However, this rapid integration has raised significant ethical concerns. Critics argue that AI systems often reflect and amplify the biases present in the data they are trained on, leading to discriminatory outcomes in areas such as hiring, lending, and law enforcement. Proponents, on the other hand, contend that with proper regulation and transparent design, AI can overcome these limitations and serve as a powerful tool for equity. The debate, therefore, is not whether AI should be used, but how it can be governed responsibly.
📄 Passage 2 — Urbanisation & the Environment
Over the past century, urbanisation has accelerated at an unprecedented rate. Today, more than half the world's population lives in cities, a figure projected to rise to nearly seventy percent by 2050. While cities are engines of economic productivity and innovation, their rapid growth has placed enormous stress on natural ecosystems. Urban sprawl reduces green spaces, increases impervious surfaces, and disrupts water cycles — collectively worsening flooding, heat islands, and biodiversity loss. Yet urban planners and environmental scientists have begun to see the city not as an adversary of nature but as an opportunity. Concepts such as green infrastructure, urban forests, and nature-based solutions are increasingly incorporated into city planning, demonstrating that sustainable urbanisation is achievable when ecological considerations are placed at the centre of development decisions.

Quick Cheat Sheet

All key rules at a glance — review this the night before the exam

⚡ Grammar — Must-Know

  • Collective nouns → singular verb
  • Each / Every / Either / Neither → singular
  • Or/Nor → verb agrees with nearest subject
  • Stative verbs (know/love/seem) → no -ing
  • Specific time (yesterday) → simple past
  • Since/For/Already → present perfect
  • Superlatives → always need "the"
  • Dangling modifier → fix the subject
  • Lists → all elements same grammatical form
  • Compare like with like → use 's for possession

📌 Prepositions — Fixed Pairs

  • Good at (skills)
  • Angry with (person) / at (thing)
  • Agree with (person) / to (proposal)
  • Arrive at (place) / in (city/country)
  • Superior to / inferior to
  • Accused of / guilty of
  • Rely on / depend on
  • Differ from / compare with
  • Capable of / incapable of
  • Interfere with

🔗 Para Jumbles — Quick Signals

  • "This/These/Such" → NEVER first
  • "However/But" → NEVER first
  • "Therefore/Hence" → NEVER first
  • "Also/Moreover" → NEVER first
  • "For example" → NEVER first
  • No intro-word → candidate for first
  • "Thus/In conclusion" → candidate for last
  • Pronoun → must follow its antecedent
  • Odd sentence → breaks the theme
  • Verify by reading the full sequence

📖 RC — Key Rules

  • Answer is always in the passage
  • Skim questions first
  • Main idea = covers whole passage
  • Inference = logical step beyond stated
  • Tone = look at adjectives + verbs
  • Vocab-in-context → substitute and check fit
  • Watch for "NOT" / "EXCEPT" in questions
  • Eliminate: too extreme, outside scope
  • No outside knowledge in RC
  • Read paragraph by paragraph, map as you go

⚠️ Confusables

  • Affect (verb) / Effect (noun)
  • Complement (complete) / Compliment (praise)
  • Principal (main) / Principle (rule)
  • Elicit (draw out) / Illicit (illegal)
  • Eminent (famous) / Imminent (about to happen)
  • Flaunt (show off) / Flout (disregard)
  • Fewer (countable) / Less (uncountable)
  • Infer (listener concludes) / Imply (speaker suggests)
  • Lay (put down) / Lie (recline)
  • Disinterested (impartial) / Uninterested (bored)

🌱 Key Word Roots

  • MAL = bad → malice, malfunction
  • CRED = believe → credible, credulous
  • RUPT = break → interrupt, disrupt
  • DICT = say → predict, contradict
  • CHRON = time → chronological
